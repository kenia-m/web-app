Our Mission

Aiming to bring a joyful holiday to children in Santiago Casandeje by delivering toys and sweets, and celebrating our cultural traditions and community.

Who We Are

Ana Machuca

Co-CEO

Upon immigrating to the United States, Ana was deeply struck by the contrast between the lives of American children and those in her hometown of Jocotitlán, Mexico. Remembering her own childhood without the joy of opening a new toy, she began collecting donations from friends, many fellow Latino immigrants like her, to bring joy to children back home.

Cultural Insight

Santiago Casandeje is a rural town in central Mexico, about a three-hour drive from Mexico City. Home primarily to indigenous Mazahua families, it has around 5,000 residents. Many children in the area do not receive toys during the holidays.

One of Mexico’s most special traditions is Día de los Reyes Magos (Three Kings Day), which commemorates the gifts brought to the newborn Jesus. Our mission is to ensure every child in Santiago Casandeje enjoys fresh Rosca de Reyes (sweet bread) and the joy of receiving a new toy on this special day.

What We Do

Rosca and Atole

The joy of receiving a toy is complemented by a warm cup of home-made atole de avena and a warm Rosca de Reyes, honoring the flavors and customs of the holiday.

Toy Drive

Each year, children of the Mazahua indigenous community receive gifts such as balls, toy cars, marbles, and even bicycles in celebration of Día de los Reyes Magos. This tradition is made possible through the generosity of our donors in the United States.

Community

Fostering community is one of the core values of Un Juguete Para Cada Niño. Through activities such as piñatas and raffles, we aim to celebrate the heart of a pueblito like Santiago Casandeje, its strong sense of togetherness.

Community Spotlight

We have stopped our work because of difficulties with immigration and traveling. However, we will continue our work through this storytelling. This section will be dedicated to showcase the resilience indigenous women in Santiago Casandeje showcase on the daily that might not otherwise be acknowledged.

In memory of Juana Hernandez Montiel, also known as Mamane, this space honors her story and legacy.

Mamane

Mamane’s life began with hardship. Her mother died during childbirth, leaving her father to raise her alone. As an Indigenous man in the early 1900s, he supported them by selling handmade wooden chairs and woven rugs (petates). At times, he traveled for a week at a time to Michoacán—sometimes by train, other times on foot—to sell his work. With no choice, he often left young Mamane at home to care for herself as early as the age of five. Since refrigerators did not exist, he stored food in a basket hung high above the ground to keep insects and animals away. She would climb on a chair to reach it and she learned to ration the food carefully to last for the entire week.

Mamane never attended school, as education for girls was uncommon and her days were filled with endless household responsibilities, specially living in a one parent household. Yet she was remarkably intelligent, fluent in both Mazahua and Spanish. During her adolescence, she worked grinding maize by hand to make tortillas—a staple food in her community. Neighbors often hired her to grind their maize using a traditional stone tool called a metate. This process was labor-intensive but completely organic: maize had to be harvested, boiled with home-made baking soda (cal), and then ground into dough. She was often seen around the village with gorgeous black long braids tied with bright, colorful ribbons.

As she grew older, Mamane married and raised five daughters. Like her father, she continued selling her own goods, walking miles to neighboring towns while carrying everything on her back in a chiquihuite (a woven basket supported by her forehead). These trips often stretched 10 kilometers each way.

Mamane’s bold spirit defined her. Once, when a skunk invaded her chicken coop in the middle of the night, she confronted it with her bare hands and, although it was a stinky fight, she won. She returned to her husband, who was asleep, and asked him to turn on the hot water so she could take a bath and rinse off. Another time, when mice tried to invade her home, she waited patiently for hours in the dark, then caught them with her sharp hands. Though denied formal education, Mamane carried immense wisdom and work ethic. She continues to be an inspiration for all the women in my family.
